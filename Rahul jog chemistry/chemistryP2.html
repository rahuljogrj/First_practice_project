Prior knowledge (5 minutes)

Separating mixtures using filtration, crystallisation, and distillation

Use the POA Getting Started activity to recap separating techniques previously studied. Ask the class to vote on each answer and then select the correct option from the dropdown menus to fill in the gap. Alternatively, you could choose a student to lead the activity.

Getting started

Option 1: Crime scene (5 minutes)

  • Show a short video clip about the use of chromatography in identifying a forgery or in identifying paint samples or other forensic evidence (search for CSI chromatography on the Internet).
  • Alternatively, ask students to suggest how different inks can be compared to help in detecting forgeries . (What could be done to find out if a pen was used in a forgery? ANS: We could analyse and compare the inks to see if they were used at the same time. Explain that chromatography is the technique we would use to do this.

Option 2: Chromatography revision (10 minutes)

  • Pre-prepare a chromatogram from a circle of filter paper by making two cuts towards the centre to make a wick.
  • Support the paper on top of a beaker of water with the wick dipping into the water and spot a coloured sweet solution or a drop of coloured ink or food colouring at the centre of the paper.
  • Wait until concentric rings of colour have formed, then remove the paper and dry it.
  • Show the chromatogram to students and ask them for key words to help in describing how this has been created. Suggested words include: dissolve, move, solvent, solution, chromatogram.
    Write the key words on the board and then challenge students to come up with sentences that explain what happened. Do they struggle to explain any of the key words?
  • You can either provide your own summary of how the chromatogram was made or ask students to keep a note of their initial efforts and add to them or amend them at the end of the lesson.

  Equipment

pre-prepared chromatogram (made using filter paper, scissors, small beaker, water, coloured sweet solution/ ink/food colouring)

Front of class 1

Chromatography

  • Depending on the time available, it is possible to spread this work over two 1-hour lessons. The first lesson should focus on the chromatography practical with the second lesson recapping the practical and calculating Rf There are additional worksheets which can be used to supplement the main activities from both lessons. If carried out in one lesson, the first half should focus on the experiment. But as the chromatogram will not dry fast enough to use to calculate Rf values, there should be previously prepared chromatograms for students to use.
  • Ask students to name mixtures of different coloured compounds. (ANS: inks, paints, and food colourings. Explain that chromatography is a technique that can be used to find out which coloured compounds a mixture contains. Explain that in this lesson you will be using ink as the main example as inks often contain a mixture of dyes.
  • Explain that paper chromatography works because some compounds dissolve more easily than others in a particular solvent. When the solvent moves up the chromatography paper, it carries the different components in the mixture at different speeds – so the components become separated. Explain that the chromatography paper is more absorbent but than regular paper, which would not work in the same way.
  • Explain that the paper showing the separated components is known as a chromatogram. Show some examples of chromatograms if not used in the starter.

Demo chromatography procedure

  • Show how to carry out a chromatography experiment. The POA Core Content 1 has a video and a set of step-by-step instructions, as does Worksheet L7a.
  • To test their knowledge of the steps in this experiment, students could complete POA Activity 1.

Front of class 2

  • This practical is one of the core pracaticals required by the specification (1.13). it is supported by the infirmation in The Student Book( pages 19-20) IG Chemistry Lab Book) and worksheet L7a which all provide instructions to help students to compare the mixtures of dyes in inks/food colourings. Have a selection of pens available, labelled with letters for ease of identification. These should be tested before the lesson to ensure that they do contain different mixtures of dyes. One set of pens will be sufficient for the whole class. Alternatively bottles of black inks from different manufacturers could be used instead of pens. If using food colourings, try to use darker colours.
  • Although timing has been given as 30 minutes, the actual experiment will take 10 minutes maximum to set up and depending on technician support it could be even quicker (technicians could be tasked with pre-cutting paper for beaker size, attaching to wooden splints and even applying pencil line and inks to a few chromatography papers as a back-up). Whilst the chromatogram is running, as much as students will enjoy watching the colours separate, it would be a good idea to go onto Rf values (see Front of class teaching 3).
  • This is a relatively simple procedure, like the chromatography investigations that students will have done during in earlier years. The second side of Worksheet L7a contains two separate prompts to help students plan a further investigation. The first suggests investigating the mixtures of dyes in inks other than black or looking at mixtures in 'permanent' inks. Students will need to use solvents other than water for this.
  • The second uses a crime scenario, asking students to test different pens to see if one of them could have been used to write a 'poison pen' letter. Have a selection of four or more pens available, and a pre-prepared chromatogram made from one of them, with the final solvent level marked to allow students to make Rf calculations from it. This is the chromatogram supposedly made using ink extracted from the letter. Also have available a short paragraph of text describing how the chromatogram was made. You could follow this up by discussing whether identifying the type of pen used for writing the letter proves that the suspect from whose home the pen was taken was the writer (no – because many other people own a similar pen; or the pen could have been left in their home by someone else).
  • The expected results are that darker inks will comprise multiple dyes. The results of the other investigations will depend on the pens available.
  • Alternatively, students could complete Worksheet L7c which reinforces the key ideas in this practical. Weaker students should answer question 1 in pairs or small groups. Check their understanding before they start. Other students can work in pairs or small groups to answer question 1 and 2 on the worksheet. Question 2 can be made a little harder by removing the word cards from the bottom of the sheet before giving it out. When they have completed the questions, check which students had difficulty with which questions and use the level of the problem to identify any areas for revisiting before moving on to the next topic.
  • Support: The further investigation looking at the range of dyes in different coloured pens is the most suitable for students working at this level. Help them to write a simple plan for their investigation, and check that their apparatus is set up correctly to obtain suitable chromatograms.
  • Stretch: If doing the ‘Poison Pen’ activity, some of the pens provided could contain water-soluble inks and some could contain permanent inks – this will allow rapid elimination of some of them. In addition, students could be provided with several types of chromatography paper, so they will need to find out the type of paper used in the pre-prepared chromatogram and use that type so that they can compare Rf values.

  Equipment

250 cm3 beaker, chromatography paper cut to fit the beaker used and stapled to a splint or attached to a pencil or glass rod using paper clips, four different black felt pens or water-soluble marker pens labelled A–D

For the chromatography investigations: selection of different coloured marker pens or felt-tip pens, selection of pens with 'permanent' (non-water-soluble) inks, solvent that will dissolve the permanent ink (for biro ink a mixture of butan-1-ol, ethanol, water (3:1:1 by volume) is suitable; and the addition of a few drops of 880-ammonia may produce a better chromatogram). For other permanent inks try ethanol, propanone or propan-2-ol, but test them before the lesson to see if they work with the pens to be used.

For the ‘Poison Pen’ investigation: selection of four or more pens of one colour (blue or black) with different combinations of dyes, labelled Suspect 1, Suspect 2 etc, pre-prepared chromatogram made using one of the pens using the same paper that students will use, suitable solvent (see above) if the pens to be tested have permanent inks.

  Safety

The solvent suggested for biro ink is flammable and harmful – ensure there are no naked flames in the lab.

Front of class 3

Rf Values

  • Explain that a visual inspection of a chromatogram is just one means of analysis, but there is a more accurate procedure using numerical values to produce an Rf value (retardation factor).
  • Using a chromatogram (preferably one produced in the previous practical, but this is unlikely to have dried sufficiently – the prudent use of a hair dryer and help with this) and a document camera or visualiser show how to calculate Rf Explain that Rf values are determined by two measurements:
    • the distance moved by the spot
    • the distance moved by the solvent.
  • POA Core Content 2 includes two sample calculations as well as a video showing how to calculate Rf values.
  • Support: Work through the calculation of Rf values with students before they attempt any questions. Discuss why it is not possible to gain a value greater than 0.
  • Stretch: Ask students what is likely to cause an Rf value of 0. (ANS: incorrect solvent for inks/dyes being analysed) Ask students why an Rf value greater than 1 is incorrect? (ANS: it would mean the ink/dye would have overtaken the solvent).
  • Students should complete an activity to apply their knowledge of Rf values, either POA Activity 2 or worksheet L7d are suitable for this.

Uses of chromatography

Explain the uses of paper chromatography. Make sure that students know to make comparisons of Rf values with published databases, all chromatograms must be produced with the same conditions (i.e., same solvent, at the same temperature etc.)

End of lesson

  • Option 1: Chromatograms (5 minutes)

Display some chromatograms on a screen and ask students to provide any information they can about them, using key words such as 'pure', 'mixture', 'similar dyes'. Search the internet for "GCSE chromatograms" for suitable images. You could even display chromatograms which have not worked properly (i.e., wrong solvent, solvent higher than pencil line, not left for long enough, left for too long). Students could then try to identify what has gone wrong.

  • Option 2: Revisit lesson objectives (5 minutes)

Display the lesson objectives. Get students to rate their confidence on each objective. Randomly select to explain their rating and for others to demonstrate their new understanding of the topic.

  • Option 3: Progression Check (10 minutes)

Ask students to complete the Assessment and Feedback sections of the Progression Check sheet for this topic. Students could feedback to each other in pairs about what they had learnt in the lesson and how they rated each statement.
